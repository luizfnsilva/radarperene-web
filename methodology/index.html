Radar Perene methodology

Radar Perene reads Brazil's market the way one reads an archive: first the regime — the prevailing state of the market over a window —, then the precedent. Where most analysis reads isolated indicators and tries to forecast, the Radar reads regimes and remembers: what was observed, what followed, and what rhymes with today. This page presents how the house reads — the axes, the cast of characters, and the principles by which fifteen years of archive become a reading.

1. What Radar Perene does, in one sentence

Radar Perene reads the state of the Brazilian market by combining three orthogonal axes (Brazil Regime, Global Regime, Intermarket BR), a set of proprietary instruments (ERP_BR, Logarithmic Regression Cone, the Ânima Index and the Perene Risk Index — the two proprietary indices stacked under every asset's price —, Historical Analogs, and turning-point Signals), and five regulatory and market lenses (Wealth, Macro/CVM/Central Bank, Institutional/Integrity, Real Estate, Electoral), plus an adjacent experiment (Lente Vértice) for cross-domain hypotheses under Bayesian discipline.

The Radar does not forecast. The Radar contextualizes. First the regime. Then the decision.

2. The three axes of the reading

The Radar's reading comes from the crossing of three independent axes. Convergences confirm; divergences organize nuance.

2.1 Brazil Regime

The Brazil Regime is the aggregate reading of Brazil's prevailing market state over a defined window, on a categorical scale (defensive, neutral, pro-risk) and an auxiliary 0–100 scale. It answers the first operational question of the day: what terrain am I standing on? Full reference: /concepts/regime-brazil.

2.2 Global Regime

The Global Regime is the aggregate reading of the external environment pressing on Brazil, on the same categorical scale as the Brazil Regime. It combines classical global-risk signals (volatility, dollar strength, US long rates, gold, defensive FX pairs) and only makes sense crossed with Brazil. Reference: /concepts/regime-global.

2.3 Intermarket BR

Intermarket BR is the cross-reading of Brazilian wealth-sector ratios (finance, utilities, commodities, REITs, cyclical/defensive, coffee/gold, VALE/gold) on a comparable scale. It is not a lens — it is the parallel layer informing all lenses. Reference: /concepts/intermarket-br.

2.4 Why the three are orthogonal

The three axes read independent dimensions. The Brazil Regime is domestic regulatory and quantitative surface; the Global Regime is external macro; Intermarket BR is domestic sectoral flow. Institutional capital often positions itself on the Intermarket axis before the regulatory axis moves — the product's canonical thesis sentence comes from that crossing: capital positioned before the headline.

The exact weighting by which the three axes combine — weights, gates, normalizations, baskets, filters — is part of the house's craft: declared in the technical architecture for audit, not exposed on the surface. But what sets the Radar apart is neither the formula nor the memory alone — it is the combination. The method lets you observe; the archive lets you compare. And these are not just any fifteen years: they are fifteen years observed through the same lens, frozen and auditable — something no one fabricates retroactively.

3. The cast — the characters of the reading

The Radar has no categories; it has recurring characters that return in every episode of the archive. Below, the cast — in two groups: the characters (the forces observed — mood, appetite, structure, the dollar, rates, cyclicals, defensives) and the instruments (the magnifying lenses over them). Each has its own concept page, with definition, example and limitation.

3.1 ERP_BR — Equity Risk Premium Brazil

ERP_BR is the differential between the aggregate earnings yield of the Brazilian equity market and the domestic ex-ante real rate, read as a historical percentile within the modern monetary regime. It solves the ruler problem: P/E alone lies, earnings yield alone lies, the real rate alone lies. ERP_BR contextualizes both sides within Brazil's own historical distribution.

Reading: low percentile → equities relatively expensive against rates; high percentile → equities relatively attractive. Not a buy recommendation — an elevated premium can stay elevated for years under structural deterioration. Reference: /concepts/erp-br.

3.2 Logarithmic Regression Cone

The Logarithmic Regression Cone frames the current price of an asset or index against its long-term historical trajectory on a logarithmic scale. The central line is the fitted trend; the bands display typical deviations calibrated by the historical standard deviation of the residual.

Reading: price above the upper band → stretched; price inside the cone → typical band; price below the lower band → contracted. Does not imply mean reversion — deviations can persist for years. Reference: /concepts/logarithmic-regression-cone.

3.3 Ânima Index

The Ânima Index is a methodological adaptation inspired by composite market-sentiment readings, adjusted to the limits and characteristics of the Brazilian market (lower depth, more concentrated volume, different seasonality from the US market). It reads the aggregate mood of an asset or index on a 0–100 scale, against the asset's own historical distribution.

Reading: extremes are not automatic reversal signals — they indicate that the current band is uncommon within the history. Ânima does not tell you what to buy. It shows when market mood is ceasing to be neutral. Reference: /concepts/anima-index.

3.4 Perene Risk Index

The Perene Risk Index is the proprietary risk-appetite gauge of the Brazilian market — risk-on / risk-off in international jargon — on a single 0–100 scale (84-day range rank), built from market breadth and cross-asset risk appetite, adapted to Brazil's characteristics. Together with the Ânima Index it forms the pair of proprietary indices stacked under every asset's price: Ânima reads mood; the Perene Risk Index reads the environment's risk appetite.

Reading: the same price move has opposite interpretations depending on the environment. Petrobras falling with the Perene Risk Index in risk-on terrain is an idiosyncratic signal; falling with the index in strong risk-off is market beta. Without the environment's tilt, the reader confuses idiosyncratic with systemic. Reference: /concepts/risk-on-risk-off.

3.5 Historical Analogs

Historical Analogs identify past windows with a regime profile similar to the present and display what followed within each — as distribution, not as forecast. For each current state, the engine searches its statistical memory for the closest windows (measured by vector distance over the reading's components) and returns: period, distance, subsequent behavior at 10, 20, 60, and 120 days, observed outcome range, and relevant divergences (what is different now).

Analogs are not forecasts. They are distribution maps. Reference: /concepts/historical-analogs.

3.6 Turning-point Signals

In risk-regime transitions — typically extreme fear followed by reversal — the engine marks dated signals on the asset's chart and shows the historical table of what followed (observed returns from one week to one year) alongside the pattern's aggregate statistics (frequency, median, dispersion). Each signal is confronted with the actual outcome as time passes.

Reading: a turning-point signal is a descriptive historical distribution of what tended to follow similar setups — never a buy or sell recommendation, nor a promise of repetition. Reference: /concepts/risk-on-risk-off.

4. The five lenses

The five principal lenses cover regulatory and market dimensions of Brazil. Each lens is fed by its own taxonomy, window, and sources; each lens returns trajectory, convergence, context window, and contextual confidence.

  • Wealth Lens — what threatens or favors the preservation of wealth. ITCMD in motion, family holdings under review, Law 14.754 and the offshore terrain, succession and asset protection. Lens page.
  • Macro / CVM / Central Bank Lens — the price of money in Brazil. Monetary policy, FX, capital markets, banking regulation, credit, fiscal. Lens page.
  • Institutional / Integrity Lens — the rules being written now. Sectoral compliance, LGPD/ANPD, anticorruption, agency-level regulation, public procurement, institutional case law. Lens page.
  • Real Estate Lens — property versus fixed income. Sectoral regulation, mortgage credit, REITs, SINAPI/FipeZap, urban legislation, municipal property tax, IMOB basket cross-check. Lens page.
  • Electoral Lens — political mood before the vote. Candidacies, campaign finance, accounting, advertising, rulings. Short windows tied to the official calendar. Lens page.

Umbrella page: /lenses.

5. The Vértice Experiment

Vértice is the Radar's methodological experiment — a Bayesian system that formulates cross-domain hypotheses between regulatory, market, crypto, sentiment, and Brazilian fragility. It does not replace the five lenses; it operates in parallel, under stricter discipline.

Every Vértice hypothesis is structured as an object: evidence, contradictions, what strengthens it, what weakens it, next signals to watch, categorical contextual confidence, and mandatory adversarial critique. Without listed evidence and contradictions, the hypothesis is rejected before it appears. Vértice formulates hypotheses; history calibrates confidence; the decision remains human.

Reference: /concepts/vertice (methodological concept) and /lenses/vertice (product page).

6. Five reading principles

These are the principles by which the method operates. Each answers a common cognitive trap in financial analysis.

6.1 Isolated indicators inform; the regime organizes. A high real rate, on its own, is a number. A high real rate at the top historical percentile, within a deteriorating fiscal regime, with defensive intermarket and stretched sentiment, is a reading. The Radar always operates in the second form.

6.2 Historical percentile is the honest ruler. Absolute values mislead when the monetary regime changed. The Radar normalizes everything as a historical percentile within a declared statistical window (Brazil's modern monetary regime, from 2000 onward).

6.3 The statistical window is the modern regime. Before the Real, hyperinflation distorts statistics. Stacking the hyperinflation tail against 2024 destroys the signal. The Radar respects the regime boundary: statistics do not cross monetary-regime boundaries. Pre-2000 events enter as qualitative reference, not as distribution points.

6.4 Visible calibration over fake numbers. When real history for a metric is insufficient, the interface shows "in calibration" and says why. The Radar prefers admitting calibration over fabricating a number.

6.5 The future is conditional scenario, not point. The Radar never says "the IBOV will close at X". It says "under current conditions, in historically similar windows, the index moved between X and Y in N of M observed cases, over a K-day window". A longer sentence, more honest, falsifiable.

7. Ten immutable integrity principles

These are the product's policy. Binding for the engine, the interface, the communication, and any integration via API or widget.

  1. The Radar does not fake confidence. Contextual confidence is categorical (low, moderate, moderate-high, high) and auditable; a low-density corpus never displays "high".
  2. The Radar does not fake dates. `publication_date = collection_date` is treated as synthetic; never presented as real publication.
  3. The Radar does not fake sources. Every metric has a declared primary source and a traceable URL; primary sources receive different treatment from press, aggregators, and news.
  4. The Radar does not fake forecasts. Only trajectories, context windows, conditional scenarios, and contextual confidence; never deterministic certainty about a future point.
  5. No computation on synthetic dates. Trajectory, regime, convergence, and forecasting are computed only on items with real, verifiable dates (R1 gate, absolute invariant).
  6. Visible calibration. Without enough history for a metric, the interface shows a calibration state — never a fabricated number.
  7. Original score immutable. The score of each item is never overwritten; the history is auditable.
  8. Adjusted score auditable. When convergence or regime adjusts a score, the adjustment is additive, capped, and justified — with reason recorded.
  9. Primary source differentiated. Courts, regulators, and self-regulators receive different treatment from press, aggregators, and news.
  10. No advice, recommendation, or automated decision. The product describes — it never prescribes. It does not issue legal, accounting, or investment advice. It does not recommend buying, selling, hiring, or restructuring.

9. The archive — what happened next

The Radar's backbone is not today's reading; it is the memory of fifteen years of readings. Every month since 2010 was read and frozen — and from that archive comes the format that names the house: what the Radar said, and what happened next. The episodes reconstruct the great regimes (the 2015 crisis, COVID, the 2022 elections), showing — with dates — what the reading got right and, above all, what it did not imply. It is memory, not prediction. Read it at /artigos and follow the daily reading at /diario.

10. Declared limits

The sophisticated reader reads this section before any other.

  • Statistical window: regime statistics begin in 2000. Pre-2000 events enter as qualitative reference, not as distribution points.
  • Case-law coverage: the engine covers clean case law from continuous RSS onward (acts after initial coverage). Deep retroactive coverage (decisions prior to the start of automated collection) is uneven — declared explicitly where relevant.
  • Brazilian volume and depth: low-liquidity assets can produce unstable readings on the Ânima Index. Without minimum volume, the instrument remains in calibration for that asset.
  • Transmission lag: Global Regime signals have variable transmission to Brazil (weeks to months). The Radar identifies lags in historically similar windows; it does not nail the destination point.
  • Does not replace a professional: the Radar does not issue legal, accounting, or investment advice. Consult a licensed professional before wealth decisions.
  • Not a financial terminal: the Radar does not offer real-time price, order book, execution, automated allocation, or trade calls.
  • Scenario, not point: every projection is conditional ("under current conditions, …"). The Radar never assigns certainty to a future point.

12. Sources

The Radar operates on primary sources and public data. Categories and representative examples below. Not exhaustive — full coverage is maintained inside the technical architecture.

  • Federal normative acts: Brazilian Official Gazette (DOU), Federal Revenue Service (RFB), National Monetary Council (CMN), Securities and Exchange Commission (CVM).
  • Central Bank normative acts: Brazilian Central Bank (BCB) resolutions, BCB SGS series, Focus survey.
  • Case law: Brazilian Supreme Court (STF), Superior Court of Justice (STJ), Superior Electoral Court (TSE), Superior Labor Court (TST), and other courts covered by the engine.
  • Legislative acts: Brazilian Chamber of Deputies, Federal Senate, relevant state assemblies (especially for ITCMD and real estate regulation).
  • Economic and market series: BCB SGS, Treasury Direct (Tesouro Transparente), ANBIMA (IMA-B), B3 (indices via ETF proxies), FipeZap, SINAPI, IBGE (CPI, industrial production, retail and services surveys, activity).
  • International series: Federal Reserve (FRED), licensed market-data providers and public exchange market-data (Binance/Bitstamp for crypto), with ETF proxies for indices and commodities, and BCB PTAX for FX. Public crypto and on-chain sources when relevant to Vértice.
  • How market data is obtained: we use end-of-day closing values only, via public or licensed APIs — no scraping or page extraction from third parties. History is stored; only the daily close is updated.
  • Legal and economic aggregators: treated as echo signal with lower confidence; they never replace primary sources.

The Radar's source policy is declared: primary sources carry greater weight than secondary sources, and every attribution is traceable in each displayed item.

13. Frequently asked questions

Q: Does Radar Perene use artificial intelligence? A: There is a technical language layer (LLM) that organizes, classifies, and narrates the outputs of the deterministic engine. The probabilistic inference layer is declared mathematics, not mysticism. The phrase "invisible AI" describes a design decision (not selling "AI" as a sales argument), not opacity in data handling.

Q: Does the Radar give investment advice? A: No. Legal, accounting, and investment advice is the regulated activity of licensed professionals. Describing a regulatory regime and a market crossing is different from advising a trade. The Radar does the first; never the second.

Q: Does the Radar forecast prices or events? A: No. The Radar operates in conditional scenario — "under current conditions, in historically similar windows, the observed outcome was Z in N of M cases". It never assigns certainty to a future point.

Q: What does "in calibration" mean? A: It means real history for the metric is insufficient to sustain the requested reading. The system prefers admitting calibration over fabricating a number.

Q: What is a market regime, in the Radar's definition? A: The prevailing state of the market over a defined window (defensive, neutral, or pro-risk), read by a combination of quantitative indicators with a declared method. Full definition at /concepts/regime-brazil.

Q: What is ERP_BR? A: Equity Risk Premium Brazil — the differential between the aggregate earnings yield of the Brazilian equity market and the domestic ex-ante real rate, read as a historical percentile within the modern monetary regime. Full definition at /concepts/erp-br.

Q: What is the Ânima Index? A: A methodological adaptation inspired by composite market-sentiment readings, adjusted to the limits and characteristics of the Brazilian market. It reads the aggregate mood of an asset or index on a 0–100 scale, against the asset's own historical distribution. Full definition at /concepts/anima-index.

Q: What is the Perene Risk Index? A: The proprietary risk-appetite gauge of the Brazilian market (risk-on / risk-off), on a 0–100 scale by 84-day range rank, stacked under every asset's price alongside the Ânima Index. In regime transitions it marks dated turning-point signals, confronted with the actual outcome. Full definition at /concepts/risk-on-risk-off.

Q: What are turning-point Signals? A: Dated marks the engine places in risk-regime transitions, paired with the historical table of what tended to follow (from one week to one year) — a descriptive distribution, never a recommendation. They appear in the per-asset reading at `/ativo/{ticker}`.

Q: What are Historical Analogs? A: Windows from the past with a regime profile similar to the present, identified by the engine and displayed as a distribution map (not as forecast). Full definition at /concepts/historical-analogs.

Q: What is Lente Vértice? A: The Radar's methodological experiment — a Bayesian system formulating cross-domain hypotheses across regulatory, market, crypto, sentiment, and Brazilian fragility.
